Intermediate Value Theorem

The Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT) is a powerful consequence of continuity. Intuitively: if you draw a continuous curve from \((a, f(a))\) to \((b, f(b))\) without lifting your pen, you must pass through every \(y\)-value between \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\).

Statement: If \(f\) is continuous on the closed interval \([a, b]\), and \(N\) is any number between \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\), then there exists at least one number \(c\) in \((a, b)\) such that:

$$f(c) = N$$

Two requirements (both are essential):

  • \(f\) must be continuous on \([a, b]\).
  • \(N\) must be strictly between \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\).

Most common use — proving a root exists. To show \(f(x) = 0\) has a solution on \([a, b]\), check that \(f\) is continuous there and that \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\) have opposite signs. Then by IVT there is some \(c\) in \((a, b)\) with \(f(c) = 0\).

Example 1: Does \(f(x) = x^3 + x - 1\) have a root in \([0, 1]\)? \(f\) is a polynomial, so continuous everywhere. \(f(0) = -1\) and \(f(1) = 1\). Since \(0\) is between \(-1\) and \(1\), IVT guarantees a \(c \in (0, 1)\) with \(f(c) = 0\).

Example 2: Show \(\cos x = x\) has a solution in \([0, 1]\). Let \(g(x) = \cos x - x\). Then \(g(0) = 1\) and \(g(1) = \cos 1 - 1 \approx -0.46\). By IVT, some \(c\) makes \(g(c) = 0\), i.e. \(\cos c = c\).

Warning: IVT does not tell you how many solutions exist, nor does it tell you the value of \(c\) — only that at least one exists.
