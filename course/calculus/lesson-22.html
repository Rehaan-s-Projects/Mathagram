Unit 3 — Differentiation Advanced

Quotient Rule

The quotient rule tells you how to differentiate a fraction of two functions \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\).

$$\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}$$

Memorable form: "low d-high minus high d-low, over low-squared."

Example 1. Differentiate \(h(x) = \dfrac{x^2}{x+1}\).

Here \(f = x^2,\ g = x+1\), so \(f' = 2x,\ g' = 1\). Then

$$h'(x) = \frac{2x(x+1) - x^2(1)}{(x+1)^2} = \frac{x^2 + 2x}{(x+1)^2}.$$

Example 2. Differentiate \(h(x) = \dfrac{\sin x}{x}\).

$$h'(x) = \frac{\cos x \cdot x - \sin x \cdot 1}{x^2} = \frac{x\cos x - \sin x}{x^2}.$$

When not to use it. Simple expressions like \(\dfrac{3}{x^2}\) can be rewritten as \(3x^{-2}\) and differentiated with the power rule — much faster.

Common pitfalls.

  • The order in the numerator matters — it's \(f'g - fg'\), not \(fg' - f'g\).
  • Don't forget to square the denominator.
  • Always simplify after applying the rule.
