📖 Imagine the Speed

Read the passage below carefully, then answer the 8 questions that follow.

📖 Reading Passage

Imagine you are driving on a long highway. You glance at your speedometer and it reads 55 mph. A minute later it says 60. Then 63. Then 64.5. Then 64.9. You are getting closer and closer to 65 mph — the speed limit posted on the sign ahead. Even if your foot never pushes the car all the way to 65, the number your speed is approaching is 65. In mathematics, we call that number the limit.

A limit does not care whether you actually reach the value. It only cares what value you are heading toward. Your speedometer could read 64.99 and then 64.999 and never quite hit 65, but mathematicians would still say the limit of your speed is 65.

Now imagine something different. Two cars are heading toward the same intersection from opposite directions. The car coming from the west is traveling at 40 mph. The car coming from the east is traveling at 60 mph. Both cars are approaching the same point, but they are approaching it at different speeds.

In calculus, this situation is called a jump discontinuity. The left-hand limit (from the west) is 40, and the right-hand limit (from the east) is 60. Because 40 ≠ 60, the two sides do not agree, and so the overall limit at the intersection does not exist.

However, if both cars approached the intersection at exactly 50 mph, then the left-hand limit and the right-hand limit would be the same. In that case, the limit does exist and it equals 50. A limit only exists when both sides agree on the same value.
