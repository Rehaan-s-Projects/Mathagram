Unit 1 — Limits

Limits at Infinity

Limits at infinity describe the end behavior of a function — what happens as \(x\) grows without bound. These limits tell us about horizontal asymptotes.

For rational functions \(\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}\), there are three cases based on the degrees of the numerator and denominator:

  • Degree of numerator < degree of denominator: The limit is \(0\).
  • Degree of numerator = degree of denominator: The limit is the ratio of leading coefficients.
  • Degree of numerator > degree of denominator: The limit is \(\pm\infty\) (no horizontal asymptote).

Example 1: Numerator degree < denominator degree.

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x + 1}{x^2 + 5} = 0$$

Example 2: Equal degrees — use leading coefficients.

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{4x^3 - x}{2x^3 + 7} = \frac{4}{2} = 2$$

Example 3: Numerator degree > denominator degree.

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^3}{x + 1} = +\infty$$

Quick trick: Divide every term by the highest power of \(x\) in the denominator. All the lower-order terms will go to zero, leaving just the leading terms.
