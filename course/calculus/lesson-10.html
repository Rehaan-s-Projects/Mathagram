Unit 1 — Limits

Limits at Removable Discontinuities

Sometimes when you try direct substitution, you get \(\frac{0}{0}\). This is called an indeterminate form, and it usually signals a removable discontinuity — a "hole" in the graph.

A removable discontinuity occurs when a factor cancels from both the numerator and denominator. The function is undefined at that point, but the limit still exists.

Strategy: Factor, cancel the common factor, then use direct substitution on the simplified expression.

Example 1:

$$\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}$$

Direct substitution gives \(\frac{0}{0}\). Factor the numerator:

$$\frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} = \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x + 2 \quad (x \neq 2)$$

Now substitute:

$$\lim_{x \to 2} (x + 2) = 4$$

Example 2:

$$\lim_{x \to 3} \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} = \lim_{x \to 3} \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = \lim_{x \to 3}(x+3) = 6$$

The key insight: the limit describes what value the function approaches, not what value it equals at that point. Even though \(f(2)\) is undefined, the limit as \(x \to 2\) is perfectly well-defined.
