Unit 4 — Implicit & Inverse

Implicit Differentiation with Trig

When the equation involves trig functions of \(y\), the chain rule bolts a \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) onto each trig derivative.

$$\frac{d}{dx}[\sin y] = \cos y\cdot\frac{dy}{dx},\quad \frac{d}{dx}[\cos y] = -\sin y\cdot\frac{dy}{dx},\quad \frac{d}{dx}[\tan y] = \sec^2 y\cdot\frac{dy}{dx}.$$

Example 1. Differentiate \(\sin y + x = y\).

$$\cos y\frac{dy}{dx} + 1 = \frac{dy}{dx} \ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{dy}{dx}(\cos y - 1) = -1 \ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-1}{\cos y - 1} = \frac{1}{1 - \cos y}.$$

Example 2. Differentiate \(y = x \cos y\).

Product rule on the right:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \cos y + x(-\sin y)\frac{dy}{dx}.$$

Collect:

$$\frac{dy}{dx}(1 + x\sin y) = \cos y \ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\cos y}{1 + x\sin y}.$$

Example 3. Differentiate \(\tan(xy) = x\).

$$\sec^2(xy)\cdot\left(y + x\frac{dy}{dx}\right) = 1.$$

Divide and solve:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\cos^2(xy) - y}{x}.$$

Tip. Keep \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) as a single "variable" while you collect terms. Only at the very end do you divide to isolate it.
