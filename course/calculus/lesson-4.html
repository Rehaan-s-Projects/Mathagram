Chapter 04

U-Substitution

U-substitution is the integration counterpart of the chain rule for derivatives. When you see a composite function inside an integral, u-substitution can simplify it into a form you already know how to integrate.

$$\int f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\, dx = \int f(u)\, du$$

The idea: choose \(u = g(x)\), compute \(du = g'(x)\,dx\), then rewrite the integral entirely in terms of \(u\). After integrating, substitute back to the original variable.

Example 1

Evaluate \(\int 2x \cdot e^{x^2}\, dx\):

  • Let \(u = x^2\), so \(du = 2x\,dx\)
  • The integral becomes \(\int e^u\, du = e^u + C\)
  • Substitute back: \(e^{x^2} + C\)

Example 2: Adjusting Constants

Evaluate \(\int x \cdot \cos(x^2)\, dx\):

  • Let \(u = x^2\), so \(du = 2x\,dx\), which means \(x\,dx = \tfrac{1}{2}\,du\)
  • The integral becomes \(\tfrac{1}{2}\int \cos(u)\, du = \tfrac{1}{2}\sin(u) + C\)
  • Substitute back: \(\tfrac{1}{2}\sin(x^2) + C\)
