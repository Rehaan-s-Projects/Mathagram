Unit 1 — Limits

Squeeze Theorem

Some limits are impossible to evaluate directly — the function oscillates wildly or defies substitution. The Squeeze Theorem (also called the Sandwich Theorem or Pinching Theorem) lets you trap the function between two simpler ones.

Here is the formal statement:

$$\text{If } g(x) \le f(x) \le h(x) \text{ near } a, \text{ and } \lim_{x \to a} g(x) = \lim_{x \to a} h(x) = L, \text{ then } \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L.$$

The idea is simple: if \(f(x)\) is always sandwiched between \(g(x)\) and \(h(x)\), and both of those converge to the same value \(L\), then \(f(x)\) has no choice — it must converge to \(L\) too.

Classic example:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} x^2 \sin\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)$$

We know \(-1 \le \sin(1/x) \le 1\), so multiplying through by \(x^2\) (which is non-negative):

$$-x^2 \le x^2 \sin\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) \le x^2$$

Both \(-x^2\) and \(x^2\) approach \(0\) as \(x \to 0\). By the Squeeze Theorem, the limit is \(\mathbf{0}\).
