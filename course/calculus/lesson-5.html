Unit 1 — Limits

Limits at Essential Discontinuities

An essential (non-removable) discontinuity occurs at a vertical asymptote — where the function blows up to \(\pm\infty\). Unlike removable discontinuities, you can't "fix" these by canceling a factor.

Example 1: Consider \(f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}\) as \(x \to 0\).

From the right (\(x \to 0^+\)), the values grow without bound: \(\frac{1}{0.1} = 10\), \(\frac{1}{0.01} = 100\), etc.

$$\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1}{x} = +\infty$$

From the left (\(x \to 0^-\)), the values plunge: \(\frac{1}{-0.1} = -10\), \(\frac{1}{-0.01} = -100\), etc.

$$\lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{1}{x} = -\infty$$

Since the one-sided limits disagree, the two-sided limit does not exist:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x} \text{ DNE}$$

Example 2: Now consider \(f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x^2}\) as \(x \to 0\).

$$\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1}{x^2} = +\infty \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{1}{x^2} = +\infty$$

Both sides agree (both go to \(+\infty\)), so we write:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x^2} = +\infty$$

Key point: When a limit equals \(\pm\infty\), the limit technically "does not exist" as a finite number, but we use the infinity notation to describe the behavior.
