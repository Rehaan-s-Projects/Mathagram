Unit 1 — Limits

Limits by Direct Evaluation

The simplest way to evaluate a limit is direct substitution. If a function \(f\) is continuous at \(x = a\), then:

$$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$$

That's it. Plug in the value and you're done. No tricks, no algebra gymnastics — just substitute.

Imagine a Roller Coaster

You're riding toward point x = a. The limit is the height you're heading toward — even if the track has a gap there!

Imagine a Mountain Landscape

As you walk along the path toward x = a, your altitude (the function value) approaches a specific height. That height is the limit!

Imagine a Speed Limit

Your car speeds up: 55 → 60 → 63 → 64.9 → getting closer and closer to 65 mph. The limit is 65 — the value you approach, even if you never quite hit it!

This works for polynomials, rational functions (when the denominator isn't zero), trig functions, and exponentials. If the function doesn't blow up or have a hole at that point, direct substitution is your move.

Example 1:

$$\lim_{x \to 3} (2x + 1) = 2(3) + 1 = 7$$

Example 2:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \cos(x) = \cos(0) = 1$$

Direct substitution is always your first attempt. Only when it produces something undefined — like \(\frac{0}{0}\) — do you need fancier techniques.
