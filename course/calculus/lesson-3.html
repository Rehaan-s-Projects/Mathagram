Chapter 03

Definite Integrals

A definite integral computes the net area between a curve and the x-axis over a specific interval \([a, b]\). Unlike indefinite integrals, definite integrals produce a number, not a family of functions.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connects differentiation and integration. It states that if \(F(x)\) is an antiderivative of \(f(x)\), then:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\, dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

Example

Let's evaluate \(\int_1^3 2x\, dx\):

  • Find the antiderivative: \(F(x) = x^2\)
  • Plug in the upper bound: \(F(3) = 9\)
  • Plug in the lower bound: \(F(1) = 1\)
  • Subtract: \(F(3) - F(1) = 9 - 1 = 8\)

Note on net area: Definite integrals give net area — regions below the x-axis count as negative. So the result is not always positive.
