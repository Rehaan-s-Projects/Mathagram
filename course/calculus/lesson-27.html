Unit 4 — Implicit & Inverse

Implicit Differentiation with Exponentials

When the equation involves \(e^y\), \(e^{xy}\), or \(\ln y\), the chain rule bolts a \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) onto each \(y\)-involving term.

$$\frac{d}{dx}[e^y] = e^y\cdot\frac{dy}{dx},\qquad \frac{d}{dx}[\ln y] = \frac{1}{y}\cdot\frac{dy}{dx}.$$

Example 1. Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) if \(e^y + x = y\).

$$e^y\frac{dy}{dx} + 1 = \frac{dy}{dx}\ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{dy}{dx}(e^y - 1) = -1\ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{1 - e^y}.$$

Example 2 — product inside an exponential. Differentiate \(e^{xy} = x + y\).

$$e^{xy}\left(y + x\frac{dy}{dx}\right) = 1 + \frac{dy}{dx}.$$

Collect the \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) terms:

$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigl(xe^{xy} - 1\bigr) = 1 - ye^{xy}\ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1 - ye^{xy}}{xe^{xy} - 1}.$$

Example 3 — logarithm of \(y\). Differentiate \(\ln y + x^2 = 2y\).

$$\frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx} + 2x = 2\frac{dy}{dx}\ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{dy}{dx}\!\left(\frac{1}{y} - 2\right) = -2x\ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-2x}{\frac{1}{y} - 2} = \frac{2xy}{2y - 1}.$$

Takeaway. Implicit differentiation is still the same two-step recipe — apply the chain rule, collect \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) terms — you just need to remember the derivatives of \(e\) and \(\ln\).
