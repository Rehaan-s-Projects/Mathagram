Unit 1 — Limits

Limits at Jump Discontinuities

Not every limit can be solved by plugging in a value. Sometimes a function jumps at a point — the left side and the right side approach different values.

We write the left-hand limit (approaching from below) and the right-hand limit (approaching from above) like this:

$$\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)$$

A two-sided limit exists only if both one-sided limits are equal:

$$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \iff \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L$$

A jump discontinuity happens when both one-sided limits exist, but they are different. The function literally jumps from one value to another.

Example: Consider the piecewise function

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} 2x & \text{if } x < 1 \\ x + 3 & \text{if } x \ge 1 \end{cases}$$

As \(x \to 1^-\), we use \(2x\): the left-hand limit is \(2\).
As \(x \to 1^+\), we use \(x + 3\): the right-hand limit is \(4\).
Since \(2 \neq 4\), the limit does not exist at \(x = 1\).

Reading Practice — Imagine the Speed

Imagine you're driving on a highway. Your speedometer reads 55 mph, then 60, then 63, then 64.5, then 64.9. You're getting closer and closer to 65 mph — that's the speed limit. Even if you never actually hit 65, the number your speed is approaching is 65. In calculus, we call that number the limit.

Now imagine something strange: two different cars on two different roads are both heading toward the same intersection. One car is approaching from the west at 40 mph. The other is coming from the east at 60 mph. They're heading to the same point, but at different speeds. This is like a jump discontinuity: the left-hand limit (from the west) is 40, and the right-hand limit (from the east) is 60. Since 40 ≠ 60, the overall limit at that intersection does not exist.

But if both cars approached at 50 mph, then the left and right limits would match. In that case, the limit does exist and equals 50. A limit only exists when both sides agree.
