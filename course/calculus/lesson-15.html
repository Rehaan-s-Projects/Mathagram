Unit 2 — Derivatives

Definition of the Derivative

The derivative of a function at a point is the instantaneous rate of change at that point. It's what you get when you take the average rate of change and let the interval shrink to zero.

Limit Definition of the Derivative:

$$f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h}$$

An equivalent form uses \(x \to a\):

$$f'(a) = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}$$

Geometric meaning: The derivative \(f'(a)\) is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of \(f\) at the point \((a, f(a))\). The tangent line just touches the curve at that point and matches its direction.

Notation: The derivative of \(f(x)\) can be written as:

  • \(f'(x)\) — Lagrange notation (prime notation)
  • \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) — Leibniz notation
  • \(\dfrac{df}{dx}\) — also Leibniz
  • \(\dot{y}\) — Newton's notation (used in physics)

Example: Find \(f'(x)\) for \(f(x) = x^2\) using the limit definition.

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h}$$
$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2xh + h^2}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} (2x + h) = 2x$$

So the derivative of \(x^2\) is \(2x\). At \(x = 3\), the slope of the tangent line is \(f'(3) = 6\).

Key insight: If a function is differentiable at a point, it must be continuous there. But being continuous does NOT guarantee differentiability (think of a sharp corner like \(|x|\) at \(x = 0\)).
