Unit 1 — Limits

Determining & Classifying Discontinuities

Now that you know the three conditions for continuity, let's classify what goes wrong when a function isn't continuous. There are three main types of discontinuity:

1. Removable Discontinuity (Hole)

The limit exists, but either \(f(a)\) is undefined or \(f(a) \neq \lim_{x \to a} f(x)\). You can "remove" the discontinuity by redefining \(f(a)\).

$$f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} \quad \text{has a removable discontinuity at } x = 1$$

2. Jump Discontinuity

The left-hand and right-hand limits both exist but are not equal. Common in piecewise functions.

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} x + 1 & x < 2 \\ x - 1 & x \geq 2 \end{cases}$$

Here \(\lim_{x \to 2^-} f(x) = 3\) but \(\lim_{x \to 2^+} f(x) = 1\). The function "jumps."

3. Infinite Discontinuity

At least one of the one-sided limits is \(\pm\infty\). This happens at vertical asymptotes.

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{x} \quad \text{has an infinite discontinuity at } x = 0$$

Classification strategy:

  • Check if the limit exists and is finite. If yes → removable.
  • Check if both one-sided limits are finite but unequal → jump.
  • If any one-sided limit is \(\pm\infty\) → infinite.
