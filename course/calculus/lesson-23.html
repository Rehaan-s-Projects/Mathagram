Unit 3 — Differentiation Advanced

Chain Rule

The chain rule tells you how to differentiate a composition — a function inside a function.

$$\big(f(g(x))\big)' = f'\!\big(g(x)\big)\cdot g'(x)$$

In Leibniz notation, if \(y = f(u)\) and \(u = g(x)\):

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx}.$$

Recipe. Differentiate the outside function (treating the inside as a blob), then multiply by the derivative of the inside.

Example 1. Differentiate \(y = (3x+1)^5\).

Outside is "something to the 5", inside is \(3x+1\). Derivative of outside: \(5(\cdot)^4\). Derivative of inside: \(3\). Multiply:

$$y' = 5(3x+1)^4 \cdot 3 = 15(3x+1)^4.$$

Example 2. Differentiate \(y = \sin(x^2)\).

$$y' = \cos(x^2)\cdot 2x.$$

Example 3. Differentiate \(y = e^{\sqrt{x}}\).

$$y' = e^{\sqrt{x}}\cdot\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}.$$

Nesting deeper. The chain rule stacks: for \(f(g(h(x)))\), \(\frac{d}{dx} = f'\!(g(h))\cdot g'(h)\cdot h'(x)\). Always work from outside in.

Common mistake. Forgetting to multiply by the derivative of the inside — "bringing down the exponent" alone isn't enough when the argument isn't just \(x\).
