Unit 2 — Derivatives

Average Rate of Change

Before we can talk about instantaneous rates of change (derivatives), we need to understand the average rate of change. It's the slope of the line connecting two points on a graph — the secant line.

$$\text{Average Rate of Change} = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}$$

This is just the slope formula from algebra: rise over run. The numerator is the change in output (\(\Delta y\)), and the denominator is the change in input (\(\Delta x\)).

The Difference Quotient: If we let the second point be \(x = a + h\) (where \(h\) is the distance between the two \(x\)-values), we get the difference quotient:

$$\frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h}$$

This form is the stepping stone to the derivative. As \(h \to 0\), the secant line becomes the tangent line.

Example 1: Find the average rate of change of \(f(x) = x^2\) from \(x = 1\) to \(x = 4\).

$$\frac{f(4) - f(1)}{4 - 1} = \frac{16 - 1}{3} = \frac{15}{3} = 5$$

Example 2: A car's position is given by \(s(t) = 3t^2 + 2t\) (in meters). Find the average velocity from \(t = 1\) to \(t = 3\).

$$\frac{s(3) - s(1)}{3 - 1} = \frac{(27 + 6) - (3 + 2)}{2} = \frac{33 - 5}{2} = \frac{28}{2} = 14 \text{ m/s}$$

The average rate of change tells you the overall trend between two points. It doesn't tell you what happens at any single instant — that's what derivatives are for.
