Unit 5 — Applications of Derivatives I

Tangent Lines

The tangent line to a curve \(y = f(x)\) at the point \(x = a\) is the straight line that touches the curve at \((a, f(a))\) and has the same slope as the curve there.

$$y - f(a) = f'(a)\,(x - a)$$

That's just point-slope form with the point \((a, f(a))\) and slope \(f'(a)\).

Three-step recipe:

  • Compute \(f(a)\) — the \(y\)-coordinate of the tangent point.
  • Compute \(f'(a)\) — the slope.
  • Plug into point-slope form.

Example 1. Find the tangent line to \(y = x^2\) at \(x = 3\).

\(f(3) = 9\) and \(f'(x) = 2x\) so \(f'(3) = 6\). Tangent:

$$y - 9 = 6(x - 3),\ \text{or}\ y = 6x - 9.$$

Example 2. Find the tangent line to \(y = \sin x\) at \(x = 0\).

\(f(0) = 0\) and \(f'(0) = \cos 0 = 1\). Tangent:

$$y = x.$$

Example 3 — horizontal tangent. \(y = x^2 - 6x + 5\) has \(f'(x) = 2x - 6 = 0\) at \(x = 3\). The tangent line there is \(y = -4\).

Tangent line as a linear approximation. Near \(x = a\), \(f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x - a)\). This is the basis of "linear approximation" or "linearization" problems — the tangent gives a simple estimate of \(f\) near \(a\).
