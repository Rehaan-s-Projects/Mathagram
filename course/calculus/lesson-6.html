Unit 1 — Limits

Limits at Jump Discontinuities

Not every limit can be solved by plugging in a value. Sometimes a function jumps at a point — the left side and the right side approach different values.

We write the left-hand limit (approaching from below) and the right-hand limit (approaching from above) like this:

$$\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)$$

A two-sided limit exists only if both one-sided limits are equal:

$$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \iff \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L$$

A jump discontinuity happens when both one-sided limits exist, but they are different. The function literally jumps from one value to another.

Example: Consider the piecewise function

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} 2x & \text{if } x < 1 \\ x + 3 & \text{if } x \ge 1 \end{cases}$$

As \(x \to 1^-\), we use \(2x\): the left-hand limit is \(2\).
As \(x \to 1^+\), we use \(x + 3\): the right-hand limit is \(4\).
Since \(2 \neq 4\), the limit does not exist at \(x = 1\).
