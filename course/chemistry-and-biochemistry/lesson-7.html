VSEPR & Molecular Geometry

VSEPR stands for Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion. This model predicts the 3D shape of a molecule by assuming that electron pairs around a central atom repel each other and arrange themselves as far apart as possible.

Electron Geometry vs Molecular Geometry

Electron geometry considers all electron pairs (bonding and lone pairs) around the central atom. Molecular geometry only considers the positions of the atoms — lone pairs are invisible in the molecular shape but still affect it.

Key Shapes

The number of electron groups around the central atom determines the electron geometry:

  • 2 groups → Linear (180°)
  • 3 groups → Trigonal planar (120°)
  • 4 groups → Tetrahedral (109.5°)
  • 5 groups → Trigonal bipyramidal (90° and 120°)
  • 6 groups → Octahedral (90°)

How Lone Pairs Change Shape

When lone pairs replace bonding pairs, the molecular geometry changes even though the electron geometry stays the same. For example, with 4 electron groups (tetrahedral electron geometry):

  • 0 lone pairs → Tetrahedral (CH&sub4;)
  • 1 lone pair → Trigonal pyramidal (NH&sub3;)
  • 2 lone pairs → Bent (H&sub2;O)

Lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs, which compresses bond angles slightly below the ideal values.
