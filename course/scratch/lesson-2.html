Created at MIT — Mitchel Resnick

Where Scratch came from and the idea behind it.

Diego: Nice work on Lesson 1! Now let's zoom out for a moment. Where did Scratch even come from? It was built at the MIT Media Lab by Mitchel Resnick's team — and the idea was bigger than just teaching kids to code.

The "Lifelong Kindergarten" idea

Mitchel Resnick leads a research group at MIT called Lifelong Kindergarten. The premise: the playful, project-based way kindergartners learn — building, making mistakes, sharing — is exactly how everyone, at any age, should learn creative, complex skills. Scratch was designed around that idea.

Scratch first launched in 2007. Resnick's design rules became famous in the world of educational software:

Low floor, high ceiling, wide walls.
— easy to start, room to grow, and lots of different things you can build.

Why blocks?

Resnick's team picked drag-and-drop blocks for a reason: blocks fit together only when they make grammatical sense. You can never write a syntax error in Scratch. That removes the punctuation and typo problems that block beginners in text languages, and lets you focus on the logic — exactly the thing you practiced in Lesson 1.

The "4 P's"

Resnick describes Scratch's design philosophy with four words:

  • Projects — learn by making things you care about
  • Passion — work on what excites you
  • Peers — share, remix, and learn together
  • Play — experiment freely and follow your curiosity

Every Scratch project you'll make in this course leans on these ideas — including the coding challenge below.

Loops Make Code Shorter

Catch the fish in 5 blocks or fewer

Same idea as Lesson 1 — but on a longer L-shaped corridor. You can solve it with twelve Move blocks plus a turn… or wrap them in Repeat to compress the program into a few blocks. Resnick's "low floor, high ceiling" right here.

Diego: The cat needs to walk 6 squares up, then turn right, then walk 6 squares across. You could stack twelve Move blocks plus a turn — or you could wrap each run of six in a single Repeat 6 Times block. That's a loop, and it's one of the four ideas behind every program ever written. Aim for 5 blocks.
Challenge: Guide the cat (🐱) up the left wall and across the top to the fish (🐟). The cat starts facing up. Par: 5 blocks.
