How the Big Bang Really Worked

Not an Explosion — an Expansion

The Big Bang wasn't an explosion in space — it was the expansion of space itself. There was no center, no edge, no "outside." Space itself stretched, carrying everything with it.

At time zero, everything in the universe was compressed into an infinitely hot, infinitely dense point called a singularity. The laws of physics as we know them break down at this point.

The First Moments

In the first second after the Big Bang, the universe was unimaginably hot. Quarks — the tiniest building blocks of matter — formed almost immediately. These quarks quickly combined to create protons and neutrons.

After about 3 minutes, the universe had cooled enough for protons and neutrons to fuse together, forming the nuclei of hydrogen and helium. This process is called nucleosynthesis. Nearly all the hydrogen and helium in the universe today was made in these first few minutes.

The Universe Becomes Transparent

For the next 380,000 years, the universe was a hot, opaque fog of particles and radiation. Then, as it cooled further, electrons combined with nuclei to form the first atoms. Light could finally travel freely. This ancient light is still detectable today — we call it the cosmic microwave background (CMB).

Stars and Galaxies Are Born

About 200 million years after the Big Bang, gravity pulled clouds of hydrogen together until they ignited — the first stars were born. These early stars were massive and short-lived.

After roughly 1 billion years, the first galaxies formed as stars clustered together under gravity.

And the universe is still expanding — and the expansion is accelerating, driven by a mysterious force we call dark energy.
