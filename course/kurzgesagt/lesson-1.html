The Scale of the Universe

How Big Is Everything?

The observable universe is 93 billion light-years across. That number is so large it barely means anything to the human brain — but let's try to wrap our heads around it anyway.

A light-year is approximately 9.46 trillion kilometers — the distance that light, the fastest thing in the universe, travels in one year. And the observable universe stretches 93 billion of those in diameter.

Earth Is Tiny

You could fit 1.3 million Earths inside the Sun. And our Sun? It's just a medium-sized star. VY Canis Majoris, one of the largest known stars, is roughly 1,500 times wider than the Sun. If you replaced the Sun with VY Canis Majoris, it would swallow the orbit of Jupiter.

Stars and Galaxies

Our galaxy, the Milky Way, contains an estimated 100 to 400 billion stars. And the Milky Way is just one galaxy among roughly 2 trillion galaxies in the observable universe.

Here's a mind-bending analogy: if the Sun were a grain of sand, the Milky Way would be about the size of a football field. And there are 2 trillion football fields' worth of galaxies out there.

The Age of Everything

The universe is approximately 13.8 billion years old. Earth, by comparison, is about 4.5 billion years old — roughly a third of the universe's age. Modern humans have existed for only about 300,000 years, a cosmic blink.
