Unit 1 — Electricity Basics

What Is Electricity?

Electricity is the flow of electric charge — the movement of tiny charged particles called electrons through a material (usually a metal wire).

Every atom contains a positively charged nucleus surrounded by negatively charged electrons. In conductors like copper and aluminum, some electrons are loosely bound and free to move from atom to atom. When we push those electrons along a wire in one direction, we call the result an electric current.

Key idea: electricity is not the wire, or even the electrons themselves — it's the motion of charge.

The three quantities you will meet first:

  • Charge (\(Q\)) — measured in coulombs (C). One electron carries a tiny negative charge of about \(1.6 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}\).
  • Current (\(I\)) — the rate at which charge flows past a point. Measured in amperes (A), where 1 A = 1 coulomb per second.
  • Voltage (\(V\)) — the "push" that drives charge through a circuit. Measured in volts (V). Think of it as electrical pressure.
$$I = \frac{Q}{t}$$

A useful analogy: think of a circuit as water in a pipe. Voltage is the water pressure, current is how fast the water flows, and a resistor is a narrow section of pipe that slows the flow.

Conductors vs insulators. Materials that let electrons move freely (copper, aluminum, gold) are conductors. Materials that hold their electrons tightly (rubber, glass, dry wood) are insulators. Between them sit semiconductors like silicon, which power every modern chip.

Conventional vs electron flow. Historically, current was defined as flowing from "+ to −" (conventional current) before electrons were discovered. In reality, electrons flow from "− to +". Both conventions are still used — circuit diagrams almost always show conventional current.

Static vs current electricity. Charge at rest is static electricity — the spark when you touch a doorknob. Charge in motion through a conductor is current electricity — the power behind every electronic device in your home.
