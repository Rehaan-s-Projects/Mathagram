Degrees and Radians

Angles can be measured in two common units: degrees and radians. A full rotation around a circle is 360° in degrees or \(2\pi\) in radians. Both systems describe the same angles — they're just different scales, like Celsius and Fahrenheit for temperature.

To convert between them, use these formulas:

$$\text{radians} = \text{degrees} \times \frac{\pi}{180}$$
$$\text{degrees} = \text{radians} \times \frac{180}{\pi}$$

Here are the key angles you should memorize:

  • \(30° = \frac{\pi}{6}\)
  • \(45° = \frac{\pi}{4}\)
  • \(60° = \frac{\pi}{3}\)
  • \(90° = \frac{\pi}{2}\)
  • \(180° = \pi\)
  • \(360° = 2\pi\)

Why radians? Radians are the "natural" unit for angles in mathematics. When we use radians, many calculus formulas become much simpler. For example, the derivative of \(\sin(x)\) is \(\cos(x)\) — but only when \(x\) is in radians. One radian is approximately 57.3°.
