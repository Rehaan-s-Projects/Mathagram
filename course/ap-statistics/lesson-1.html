Categorical vs Quantitative Data

What Are Data Types?

In statistics, every piece of data falls into one of two broad categories: categorical or quantitative.

Categorical Data

Categorical data represents categories or labels. Think of things like colors, yes/no answers, or types of food. You can count how many fall into each category, but you can't meaningfully add or average them.

Categorical data is further divided into two subtypes:

  • Nominal — No natural order. Examples: eye color (blue, green, brown), favorite sport (soccer, basketball, tennis).
  • Ordinal — Has a natural order. Examples: movie ratings (1-5 stars), education level (high school, bachelor's, master's).

Quantitative Data

Quantitative data represents numbers you can measure. You can add, subtract, average, and perform calculations on these values. Examples: height, weight, age, temperature.

Quantitative data also has two subtypes:

  • Discrete — Countable values, often whole numbers. Examples: number of siblings, number of pets, number of cars.
  • Continuous — Measurable values that can take any value in a range. Examples: height (5.7 feet), weight (152.3 lbs), temperature (98.6°F).
Key Insight: Just because something looks like a number doesn't make it quantitative! Phone numbers, zip codes, and jersey numbers are all categorical — you wouldn't average them.
