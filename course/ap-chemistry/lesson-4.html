Quantum Numbers

Every electron in an atom is described by a unique set of four quantum numbers. Together, these numbers specify the electron's energy, the shape and orientation of its orbital, and its spin direction.

Principal Quantum Number (\(n\))

The principal quantum number \(n\) describes the energy level (shell) of the electron. It takes positive integer values: \(n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\). Higher values of \(n\) mean the electron is farther from the nucleus and has more energy.

Angular Momentum Quantum Number (\(l\))

The angular momentum quantum number \(l\) describes the shape of the subshell. For a given \(n\), \(l\) ranges from \(0\) to \(n - 1\). Each value corresponds to a subshell type:

  • \(l = 0\) → s subshell (spherical)
  • \(l = 1\) → p subshell (dumbbell)
  • \(l = 2\) → d subshell (cloverleaf)
  • \(l = 3\) → f subshell (complex)

Magnetic Quantum Number (\(m_l\))

The magnetic quantum number \(m_l\) describes the orientation of the orbital in space. It ranges from \(-l\) to \(+l\), giving \(2l + 1\) possible orbitals per subshell. For example, when \(l = 2\) (d subshell), \(m_l = -2, -1, 0, +1, +2\) — five orbitals.

Spin Quantum Number (\(m_s\))

The spin quantum number \(m_s\) describes the spin direction of the electron. It can only be \(+\tfrac{1}{2}\) or \(-\tfrac{1}{2}\). Each orbital holds at most two electrons with opposite spins.

Pauli Exclusion Principle

No two electrons in the same atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers. This means each orbital can hold a maximum of two electrons, and they must have opposite spins.
