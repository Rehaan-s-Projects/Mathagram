Photoelectron Spectroscopy (PES)

Photoelectron spectroscopy is an experimental technique that provides direct evidence for electron configurations and energy levels within atoms. It works by shooting high-energy photons at atoms and measuring the kinetic energy of the ejected electrons.

How PES Works

When a photon with known energy hits an atom, it can knock out an electron. The binding energy of that electron is calculated using the equation:

$$E_{\text{binding}} = E_{\text{photon}} - E_{\text{kinetic}}$$

Electrons closer to the nucleus are held more tightly, so they have higher binding energies. Valence electrons, being farthest from the nucleus, have the lowest binding energies.

Reading a PES Spectrum

A PES spectrum plots binding energy on the x-axis (decreasing from left to right) and the relative number of electrons on the y-axis. Each peak corresponds to a subshell:

  • Peaks on the left (high binding energy) represent inner-shell (core) electrons
  • Peaks on the right (low binding energy) represent valence electrons
  • The height of a peak tells you how many electrons are in that subshell

Example: Nitrogen

Nitrogen has 7 electrons with the configuration \(1s^2\,2s^2\,2p^3\). Its PES spectrum shows three peaks:

  • A small peak at high binding energy → 1s² (2 electrons)
  • A medium peak → 2s² (2 electrons)
  • A taller peak at low binding energy → 2p³ (3 electrons)

By analyzing these peaks, we can determine the electron configuration and confirm the identity of the element.
