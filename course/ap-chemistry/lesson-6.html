Lewis Structures

Lewis structures (also called Lewis dot structures) show how valence electrons are arranged in a molecule. They help us predict bonding, lone pairs, and molecular shape.

Drawing Lewis Structures

Follow these steps to draw a Lewis structure:

  1. Count total valence electrons for all atoms in the molecule
  2. Draw single bonds from the central atom to each surrounding atom
  3. Complete octets on the outer atoms first (hydrogen only needs 2)
  4. Place remaining electrons on the central atom
  5. If the central atom lacks an octet, form double or triple bonds by sharing lone pairs from outer atoms

The Octet Rule

Most atoms in molecules want 8 electrons around them (an octet). Hydrogen is the exception — it only needs 2 electrons (a duet). Some elements in Period 3 and beyond can have expanded octets because they have access to d orbitals.

Lone Pairs vs Bonding Pairs

A bonding pair is a pair of electrons shared between two atoms (shown as a line). A lone pair is a pair of electrons that belongs to only one atom (shown as two dots). Both types are important for determining molecular geometry.

Example: Carbon Dioxide (CO&sub2;)

Carbon has 4 valence electrons; each oxygen has 6. Total = \(4 + 2(6) = 16\) valence electrons. Carbon is central, and single bonds alone don't satisfy octets, so we form two double bonds: O=C=O. Each oxygen also has 2 lone pairs.
