Unit 1 — Limits

Limits at Infinity

So far we've looked at what happens as \(x\) approaches a finite number. But what happens when \(x\) itself flies off toward \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\)? That's exactly what limits at infinity describe.

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L$$

This means that as \(x\) grows without bound, the function values get closer and closer to some finite number \(L\). When this happens, the line \(y = L\) is called a horizontal asymptote.

Key rules for rational functions \(\displaystyle\frac{a_n x^n + \cdots}{b_m x^m + \cdots}\):

  • If the degree of the numerator \(n\) is less than the degree of the denominator \(m\), the limit is \(0\).
  • If the degrees are equal (\(n = m\)), the limit is \(\displaystyle\frac{a_n}{b_m}\) (ratio of leading coefficients).
  • If the degree of the numerator is greater than the denominator, the limit is \(\pm\infty\) (no horizontal asymptote).

Example 1:

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^2 + 1}{5x^2 - 2} = \frac{3}{5}$$

Same degree top and bottom, so we take the ratio of leading coefficients.

Example 2:

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{4x}{x^3 + 1} = 0$$

The denominator grows much faster, so the fraction shrinks to zero.

Example 3:

$$\lim_{x \to -\infty} e^x = 0$$

As \(x\) heads to negative infinity, \(e^x\) decays toward zero. So \(y = 0\) is a horizontal asymptote on the left.
