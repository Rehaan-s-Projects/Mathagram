Unit 1 — Limits

Squeeze Theorem

Sometimes you can't evaluate a limit directly because the function is too wild — it oscillates or behaves unpredictably. The Squeeze Theorem (also called the Sandwich Theorem) lets you trap the function between two simpler ones.

The Squeeze Theorem: If for all \(x\) near \(a\) (except possibly at \(a\) itself):

$$g(x) \le f(x) \le h(x)$$

and

$$\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = \lim_{x \to a} h(x) = L$$

then

$$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$$

Think of it like this: if you're squeezed between two friends who are both walking toward the same door, you have no choice but to end up at that door too.

Classic Example: Prove \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} x^2 \sin\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) = 0\).

We know \(-1 \le \sin\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) \le 1\) for all \(x \neq 0\). Multiply through by \(x^2\) (which is non-negative):

$$-x^2 \le x^2 \sin\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) \le x^2$$

Since \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} (-x^2) = 0\) and \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} x^2 = 0\), by the Squeeze Theorem:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} x^2 \sin\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) = 0$$

Another important result from the Squeeze Theorem:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$$

This limit is fundamental and will appear constantly in calculus.
